Drawing

The foundation of seeing

Before colour, before tone, before technique — there is line. Drawing teaches you to see structure, proportion, and gesture. These principles apply whether you work in graphite, charcoal, ink, or digital.

✎
01 Beginner
Start with gesture, not detail
The biggest mistake beginners make: starting with the eyes, nose, or perfect little details. Start with gesture — the flow and movement of the whole form. Capture the energy first. Refine later.
3 min read

Think of gesture drawing as handwriting for artists. You wouldn't write a word letter by letter from the top down — you'd flow through it. Same with drawing. Capture the sweeping motion of a pose in 30 seconds. Then add structure. Then detail.

Proko - Figure Drawing Fundamentals Circle Line Art School - Gesture Drawing
02 Beginner
Draw what you see, not what you think you see
Your brain has a symbol for "eye" and a symbol for "hand." When you draw from memory, you draw those symbols. When you draw from observation, you draw the actual thing — and it looks real.
5 min read

Betty Edwards' "Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain" proves this: when students draw an image upside down, they draw it better. Why? Because they can't recognise what it is. They're forced to draw the actual shapes and angles, not their mental shorthand.

Sycra - Drawing Fundamentals Will Kemp Art School - Observational Drawing
03 Intermediate
Master the measuring techniques: sight-size and comparative measurement
Hold your pencil at arm's length. Use your thumb to measure one part of your subject. Compare that measurement to another part. This is how the old masters achieved accurate proportion.
6 min read

Sight-size drawing means your drawing is the same size as what you see when you look at your subject. Stand at your easel, look at the model (or still life), then look at your drawing without moving your head. If the drawing and the subject occupy the same visual space, proportions become obvious.

Schaefer Fine Art - Measuring & Proportion Draw Mix Paint - Classical Drawing Methods
04 Intermediate
Learn to see the negative space
Stop drawing the object. Draw the space around it. Negative space doesn't lie — your brain does. When you draw the gaps between things, proportion falls into place.
4 min read

When drawing a chair, don't draw the legs and the back. Draw the odd-shaped space between the legs and around the back. It forces you to see actual shapes instead of "chair parts." The result: accurate, believable drawings.

Emmy Kalia - Negative Space Drawing Ran Art Blog - Drawing Techniques
Featured Advanced
Study the Bargue drawing method for precision and form
The Bargue Drawing Course — used at the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris — trains you to see form, proportion, and value with ruthless accuracy. It's slow. It's deliberate. It works.
8 min read

Charles Bargue created lithographs of plaster casts and classical sculptures. Students would copy them meticulously, training their eye to see angles, proportions, and tonal relationships. It's not creative. It's training. And every atelier worth its salt still uses it.

Degas, Van Gogh, and Picasso all trained with Bargue drawings. The method isn't about self-expression — it's about precision. Once you can draw what you see with total accuracy, then you can break the rules intentionally.

Painting

Colour, tone, and light

Painting is drawing with colour. It's also light, atmosphere, texture, and mood. Whether you work in oils, acrylics, watercolour, or gouache — the fundamentals remain.

✦
01 Beginner
Value is more important than colour
A painting with correct values and wrong colours will read as believable. A painting with correct colours and wrong values will look flat and unconvincing. Get your lights and darks right first.
4 min read

Squint at your subject. The details disappear, but the big shapes of light and dark remain. Those shapes are your value structure. Paint those first — even as a monochrome underpainting — then add colour on top.

Will Kemp Art School - Value & Tone Paint Coach - Understanding Values
02 Beginner
Limited palettes force you to mix, not match
Start with just three colours plus white: a warm and cool version of red, yellow, and blue. You'll learn colour theory faster than any book could teach you.
5 min read

The Zorn palette (yellow ochre, ivory black, vermillion, white) produces incredible range. The reason: you're forced to understand warm vs cool, saturation, and how colours interact. Buying 40 tubes won't make you a better painter. Mixing will.

My Modern Met - Painting Tutorials The Paint Basket - Colour Mixing
03 Intermediate
Work fat over lean (for oils) or thin to thick (for acrylics)
Oils: each successive layer should have more oil than the last. Acrylics: start transparent, build to opaque. This prevents cracking, ensures proper drying, and gives you control over luminosity.
6 min read

Oil paint dries from the outside in. If a lean (less oil) layer sits on top of a fat (more oil) layer, the top dries faster and cracks. The rule: turpentine for your first layers, then add linseed oil progressively. Your painting will last centuries.

Draw Mix Paint - Oil Painting Techniques EmptyEasel - Oil & Acrylic Guides
04 Intermediate
Use glazing for depth and luminosity
A glaze is a transparent layer of colour over a dry layer. It shifts hue, deepens shadows, and creates an inner glow that opaque paint can't achieve. The Old Masters built entire paintings this way.
7 min read

Vermeer's luminous skin tones, Rembrandt's glowing fabrics — both used glazing. Mix your paint thin with medium (for oils) or glazing medium (for acrylics). Apply it over a dry layer. The underlayer shows through, optically mixing with the glaze. The result: depth you can't get any other way.

Jill Poyerd Fine Art - Glazing Techniques James Gurney - Color & Light
Featured Advanced
Study Munsell and the colour sphere to truly understand hue, value, and chroma
The Munsell colour system organises colour into three dimensions: hue (the colour itself), value (light to dark), and chroma (saturation). Once you understand this, colour mixing becomes logical instead of guesswork.
10 min read

Imagine a sphere. The vertical axis is value — white at the top, black at the bottom. Around the equator are the pure hues at maximum chroma. As you move toward the centre, chroma decreases (colours become greyer). Every possible colour exists somewhere on this sphere.

Understanding Munsell means you stop saying "make it more blue" and start saying "increase the chroma of this hue while keeping the value constant." It's the difference between hoping and knowing.

Photography

Light, time, and vision

Photography is about seeing, timing, and controlling light. Whether you shoot film or digital, manual or automatic — understanding exposure, composition, and light will transform your images.

◉
01 Beginner
The exposure triangle: aperture, shutter speed, ISO
Every photo is the result of three settings working together. Aperture controls depth of field. Shutter speed controls motion. ISO controls sensitivity. Change one, and you must compensate with the others.
5 min read

Wide aperture (f/1.8) = blurry background, more light. Narrow aperture (f/16) = sharp background, less light. Fast shutter (1/1000s) = frozen motion, less light. Slow shutter (1/30s) = motion blur, more light. High ISO (3200) = grainy, very sensitive. Low ISO (100) = clean, less sensitive. Master this, and you control everything.

Chris Bray Photography - Free Beginner Course PhotoRec TV - Exposure Fundamentals
02 Beginner
Golden hour is real — and it matters
The hour after sunrise and the hour before sunset. The light is warm, soft, and directional. Shadows are long and flattering. Midday sun is harsh and unflattering. Plan accordingly.
3 min read

Golden hour light is low and angled, which means it wraps around your subject instead of blasting it from above. Skin looks warm and glowing. Landscapes get depth and dimension. It's the easiest way to instantly improve your photos — just shoot at the right time.

iPhotography - Lighting Tutorials AdoramaTV - Natural Light Photography
03 Intermediate
Shoot in RAW, not JPEG
JPEG throws away 90% of your image data to save space. RAW keeps everything — which means you can recover blown highlights, lift shadows, and adjust white balance in post without destroying the image.
4 min read

Think of JPEG as a finished painting and RAW as all the paint still on your palette. Once a JPEG is saved, you can't undo the baked-in contrast, saturation, or sharpening. With RAW, you have full control. Yes, the files are bigger. Yes, it's worth it.

CreativeLive - Photography Fundamentals SLR Lounge - RAW vs JPEG
04 Intermediate
Learn to read a histogram
The histogram shows the distribution of tones in your image. Bunched up on the left = underexposed. Bunched up on the right = overexposed. A good exposure uses the full range without clipping either end.
5 min read

Your camera's LCD screen lies — it looks bright in the dark and dim in the sun. The histogram doesn't lie. If you see a spike touching the right edge, you've blown your highlights (unrecoverable). Touching the left edge means crushed blacks (also bad). Aim for a smooth curve using the full range.

ProEdu - Histogram & Exposure B&H Photo Video - Technical Guides
Featured Advanced
Master the Zone System for ultimate tonal control
Ansel Adams' Zone System divides the tonal range into 11 zones, from pure black (Zone 0) to pure white (Zone X). By pre-visualising which zone each part of your scene should fall into, you expose and develop for exactly the result you want.
12 min read

The Zone System is about control. You meter the shadows and decide: do I want detail there, or pure black? You meter the highlights and decide: textured white, or blown out? Then you expose for the shadows and develop for the highlights (or in digital, you expose to the right and bring down the highlights in post).

Adams: "You don't take a photograph, you make it." The Zone System is the technical foundation of that philosophy — every creative choice backed by intentional exposure.

Composition

The grammar of every image

Composition applies to every medium equally. Whether you're arranging a canvas, deciding where to place a subject in a frame, or positioning elements in a drawing — these principles are universal and timeless.

⬡
01 Beginner
Rule of thirds — and when to break it deliberately
Placing your subject at the intersection of thirds creates dynamic tension. But a centred subject in a symmetrical composition creates stillness and confrontation. Both are correct. Know which effect you want before choosing where to place your subject.
4 min read

Rules of thirds: dynamic, restless, directionality. Central placement: confrontational, still, meditative. The mistake is not knowing which you've chosen — the worst images are accidentally centred rather than deliberately so.

02 Beginner
Leading lines: directing the eye through the image
Lines within an image — roads, rivers, fences, shadows — direct the eye towards or away from your subject. Used intentionally, they give the viewer a path through the picture and create a sense of depth and movement.
3 min read

Leading lines work best when they start near a corner of the image and lead towards the subject or into the distance. Converging lines (a road receding to a vanishing point) create powerful depth. Diagonal lines suggest movement. Curves suggest flow.

03 Intermediate
Positive and negative space as compositional forces
Negative space is not empty — it is weight, silence, and breathing room. A subject surrounded by negative space feels isolated, contemplative, small. A subject that fills the frame feels powerful, immediate, overwhelming. Both are choices.
4 min read

Japanese aesthetics have a word for the intentional use of empty space: ma. The emptiness is not absence — it's presence. Look at minimalist Japanese woodblock prints, the photography of Hiroshi Sugimoto, or the paintings of Giorgio Morandi to understand how much a little space can say.

04 Intermediate
Framing within the frame: using architecture and environment
Using elements within your scene — doorways, windows, branches, arches — to create a secondary frame around your subject adds depth, context, and draws attention. It's one of the oldest compositional devices in painting and photography.
4 min read

The framing element doesn't need to be in sharp focus — a blurred doorway in the foreground still creates a sense of depth and leads the eye to the subject beyond it. The key is that the secondary frame draws attention inward, not outward.

Featured Advanced
Dynamic symmetry and the root rectangles: what the masters actually used
The golden ratio and rule of thirds are simplifications of a richer compositional tradition. Hambidge's dynamic symmetry — the system used by Vermeer, Raphael, and Hogarth — is based on the proportional relationships between rectangles generated from the diagonal.
10 min read

At its simplest: draw a diagonal through your canvas from corner to corner. Vertical and horizontal lines from that diagonal to the edges create harmonic divisions. Place your key elements at these intersections and the result has a quality of rightness that rules of thirds can't quite achieve.

You don't need to understand the mathematics to use dynamic symmetry. Start by drawing the diagonal of your format and seeing what natural divisions it suggests. Let the format itself guide the composition.
